## Slide 3 - The forecast is not noisy. It is biased.

**Section label:** Diagnosis

**Title:** The forecast is not noisy. It is biased.

**Hero stat:** 6 of 6 misses in the same direction

**Body:** Random error goes both ways. Same-direction misses, getting worse, usually mean the model is blind to known drivers: vendor bills, bonus timing, how clients actually pay, and how delivery data reaches finance.

**Positive turn:** That is good news. Bias is fixable in weeks. Noise is not.

**Small caption:** Cash forecast miss vs actual, last six months.

**Speaker note:** I am not criticising the team. I am saying the model is missing known operating facts. That is much easier to fix than random unpredictability.

## Slide 9 - What fixed-price actually changes

**Section label:** The commercial shift

**Title:** What fixed-price actually changes

| Area | Billing by the hour today | Fixed price ambition | Touches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | Hours billed equals revenue. Simple. | Revenue follows project progress, which depends on estimates being honest. | Forecasting |
| Cash | Invoice after the work, then wait. | Deposits and milestone payments: cash arrives before the work, not after. | Collections |
| Risk | Overruns are the client's cost. | Overruns are our margin. Two poor projects can lose 20+ points. | Margin |
| Data | Timesheets exist to bill. | Timesheets control cost: hours left, work in progress and both models side by side. | Data accuracy |
| Pricing | Rate card. | Risk-priced: less certain estimate, higher price. Margin managed across the portfolio. | Margin |

**Bottom line:** Done right, fixed-price helps the cash problem. A 30% deposit on a €300k deal is €90k in the bank before an hour is worked.

**Speaker note:** I would not frame fixed-price as a finance objection. It is commercially attractive. But it needs guardrails or the downside lands in margin.

## Slide 10 - The ambition is right. The sequencing is the risk.

**Section label:** Days 61-90 - Discipline

**Title:** The ambition is right. The sequencing is the risk.

**Opening:** The fixed-price ambition is right. The risk is moving faster than the data can support.

**Four guardrails:**
- Scoping template: deliverables, acceptance criteria and exclusions in writing.
- Risk-priced buffer: uncertainty is charged for, not absorbed.
- Mandatory change orders: scope moves, price moves; signed before extra work starts.
- Margin tripwire: weekly review of hours left versus money left.

**Readiness gate:** No new fixed-price deal above €150k until we have eight weeks of trusted utilisation data agreed with delivery.

**Bottom line:** The gate is not a brake. It is what makes 40-50% fixed-price possible without making it fatal.

**Speaker note:** I would show support for the commercial ambition, but I would insist on sequencing. Fixed-price without trusted delivery economics is not strategy; it is risk transfer to the company.

## Slide 12 - What I commit to, and what I refuse to promise

**Section label:** Day 90

**Title:** What I commit to, and what I refuse to promise

**Live by day 90:**
1. 13-week cash view: updated weekly, accuracy tracked, misses shrinking.
2. Receivables under management: five groups, one owner each, decisions executed on both 90+ day disputes.
3. Scorecard v2: eight numbers, each with one definition, one source and one owner.

**Explicitly not in 90 days:**
- No new ERP or system purchases: the problem is definitions and ownership, not software.
- No real-time everything: weekly and trusted beats real-time and disputed.
- No fixed-price at 40% of revenue: the readiness gate comes first.
- No hiring requests before the diagnosis: by day 90, the FP&A question gets answered with evidence, not instinct.

**Bottom line:** I commit to artefacts, not outcomes I do not control. I cannot promise that a client pays; I can promise the decision about that client is made and executed.

**Speaker note:** This is the credibility slide. It shows ambition without overpromising.

## Slide 13 - Three asks. None of them is budget.

**Section label:** The ask

**Title:** Three asks. None of them is budget.

**Ask 1:** 30 minutes a week.
CEO joins the weekly Cash Council for the first eight weeks. Decisions get made where the cash picture is.

**Ask 2:** A mandate on the two disputes.
Authority to settle or escalate the 90+ day disputes, including accepting a commercial haircut for speed.

**Ask 3:** Delivery co-owns utilisation.
The definition is agreed jointly. A number only finance believes is a number nobody uses.

**Closing line:** Give me those three and by day 90 confidence stops being a word on a slide. It becomes a forecast you believe, a receivables book with owners and a fixed-price model you can scale without flinching.

**Speaker note:** This makes the board part of the solution without asking for budget. That is powerful.
